Scouting Tips

How to find and hunt Washington's public land

📱

Download offline maps before you go

Cell service is unreliable in most WA hunting areas. Use Gaia GPS or OnX Hunt with maps downloaded.

🌅

Hunt transition zones at dawn and dusk

Edges between forest and open meadow — where brush meets a creek — hold the most deer.

🔭

Glass before you walk

Use binoculars to spot animals from a distance. Moving into an area spooks game. Sit and watch first.

💨

Wind matters more than anything

Always approach from downwind. Deer will smell you at 300+ yards.

⏰

First and last hour is prime time

Big game moves most at dawn and dusk. Be in position before first light.

Decide

Before you pick up a rifle, you must pick a pursuit. This is the most important decision because it determines your license, gear, season, and location. Don't overthink it — most beginners start with one of three animals.

General Cost to Get Started

forest

Big Game — Deer, Elk, Bear

Most expensive to start. Rifle + optic alone runs $500–$1,000+. Add license ($45), deer tag ($45), and mandatory gear (orange, knife, game bags). Budget $800–$1,500 for your first year. Payoff: a full freezer of wild meat.

flutter_dash

Birds — Pheasant, Grouse, Turkey, Duck

Mid-range start. A used shotgun runs $300–$500. Add upland bird license (~$40), shells, and boots. Budget $500–$900 total. Fast-paced action and excellent table fare. Waterfowl adds decoy costs ($200–$500).

cruelty_free

Small Game — Rabbit, Squirrel, Coyote

Cheapest way in. A .22 rifle costs $250–$350. Small game license is ~$35. Budget $300–$500 to start. Best choice for learning fundamentals before committing to bigger game.

02

Educate

Washington requires anyone born after Jan. 1, 1972, to complete a hunter education course. This is the foundation of safety and ethics.

⏱ What to Expect — Time Commitment

The online portion takes about 10 hours of coursework. Most people do roughly an hour a day — so plan for 2 weeks to complete it without rushing.

After that comes a 4-hour in-person field day — you shoot a firearm, demonstrate safety skills, and get signed off by an instructor. These field days fill up fast and need to be scheduled at least 2–3 weeks in advance.

👉 Bottom line: Start the certification process at least 1 month before you plan to buy your license.

03

License

Once certified, you must purchase the correct licenses and tags. Tags are species-specific.

License to Hunt Everything

A base WA Hunting License lets you hunt most species. You then add species-specific tags on top (deer tag, elk tag, etc.). This is what most hunters get — one license, multiple species.

License for One Animal

If you only plan to hunt small game or upland birds, you can purchase just a Small Game License or Upland Bird Validation — cheaper than the full bundle.

⚠️ Bag Limits — Know Before You Go

Every tag comes with limits on how many animals you can take. Deer: typically 1 per season. Pheasant: 3 roosters per day. Duck: 7 per day with species restrictions. Violating bag limits is a serious offense — always read your tag rules.

Important: Your first license must be purchased in person at an on-site location.

assignment_late

Key Dates

Most tags must be purchased by late October. Check the WDFW hunting calendar for exact deadlines.

The Armory

Choosing Your First Gun

🦌 Deer / Big Game → Bolt-action Rifle

Best starter: Savage Axis II (~$400) or Ruger American (~$450). Get it in .308 Win or .243 Win — both are widely available, manageable recoil, deadly on deer.

🐦 Birds / Turkey → Shotgun

Best starter: Mossberg 500 (~$380) or Remington 870 (~$400). A 12-gauge handles everything from pheasant to turkey. Get a modified choke for versatility.

🐇 Small Game / Rabbit → .22 Rifle

Ruger 10/22 (~$300) is the classic. Cheap to shoot, low recoil, perfect for learning fundamentals before moving up to big game.

🎯 Practice Before the Season — This is Non-Negotiable

Don't wait until opening day to shoot your gun. Get to a range at least 4–6 weeks before the season opens to sight in your scope and confirm your rifle is zeroed at the distance you plan to shoot. A gun that isn't properly sighted in is the #1 cause of wounded and lost animals. Shoot at least 20–30 rounds to verify your zero and build confidence. If you're new to firearms, consider a basic rifle or shotgun course at a local range first.

05

Scout

"A mile of scouting is worth ten miles of hiking during the season."

e-Scouting

OnX Hunt — Most Important App

Shows public vs. private land boundaries in real time using your GPS. The #1 tool to avoid trespassing. ~$30/year. Download offline maps before leaving cell service.

Gaia GPS — Navigation

Download full WA topo maps offline. Works without cell service. Know how to get back to your car even if you get turned around in the timber.

WA DNR Road Map — Timber Roads

Go to dnr.wa.gov/programs-initiatives/recreation/find-dnr-lands to see which roads are open and which require a Discover Pass or Green Dot permit. Or download Avenza Maps (free app) and add the WA DNR public lands layer for offline use.

Boots on the Ground

Visit your spots in late summer. Look for tracks, scat, and bedding areas.

A word of honesty for new hunters: Watching videos online teaches you what to look for — but your eyes won't recognize it in the field until you've seen it in person several times. Tracks, rubs, scrapes, and beds look very different in real terrain than in a YouTube thumbnail. Plan to visit your scouting location at least 2–3 times before the season opens, ideally starting 6–8 weeks out. Each visit you'll notice more than the last. There is no shortcut for time spent in the field.

WDFW Wildlife Areas

Managed specifically for public hunting. No permission needed. Signs mark the boundaries.

National Forests

Millions of acres. Great for deer, elk, grouse. Requires downloaded maps.

DNR Timber Lands

Huge clearcut areas, excellent for western WA black-tail deer.

BLM Land

Common in eastern WA. Open sagebrush country great for birds and mule deer.

06

Hunt

The culmination of your preparation. Focus on the wind, stay quiet, and be patient.

Morning of Your Hunt

① Check the WA hunting regulations for your GMU at wdfw.wa.gov
② Confirm your license and tag are in your pack
③ Tell someone where you're going and when you'll be back
④ Arrive at your spot before first legal shooting light (30 min before sunrise)
⑤ Move slowly, stay quiet, and watch more than you walk
⑥ If you harvest an animal — tag it immediately before moving it

After a Successful Harvest

• Tag the animal immediately (attach physical tag)
• Field dress as soon as possible to cool the meat
• Report your harvest at fishhunt.dfw.wa.gov within 10 days (required by law)
• Process at home or take to a local butcher ($150–400 for deer)

First Hunt Tip

Don't go alone your first time if you can help it. Ask an experienced hunter to come with you — even one trip with a mentor teaches you more than 10 hours of reading.

Food, Water, & Waste

2–3 Liters of Water

Dehydration causes poor decisions. WA streams carry Giardia — always filter or bring your own.

High-calorie snacks

2,000–3,000 calories if you're hiking hard. Jerky, nuts, energy bars. Eat before you get hungry.

Biodegradable toilet paper

Leave No Trace standard for WA backcountry. Bury waste 6 inches deep and 200 feet from water.

After using the bathroom and before handling food. Essential hygiene in the field.

Pack it in, pack it out. Also useful for keeping wet gear separated or covering your pack in rain.

emergency_share

Safety & Response

First Aid Kit

See the First Aid Kit tab for the full 30-item checklist. Build it once, carry it every hunt. Non-negotiable.

Headlamp + Spare Batteries

You will be hiking in the dark — either before dawn or packing out after a late kill. Always have this. Cold kills battery life fast, so carry spares inside a warm pocket.

Fire Starter Kit

Carry at least two methods: lighter + waterproof matches + ferro rod. WA wilderness can turn dangerous fast. Fire means warmth, signal, and survival. Petroleum jelly-soaked cotton balls make excellent tinder.

Satellite Communicator (Garmin inReach / SPOT)

Highly recommended for WA hunting. Most backcountry areas have zero cell coverage and can be hours from the nearest town. A Garmin inReach Mini runs ~$35/month and can summon rescue from anywhere on Earth.

Portable Power Bank

Keep your phone, GPS, and satellite communicator charged in the field. A 10,000–20,000mAh bank will last multiple days. Cold temperatures drain batteries quickly — keep it warm in your pack.

Camera / Phone

WA requires photo documentation for some special permit harvests. Beyond that — you'll want the memories. Keep your phone in a waterproof case and download offline maps before you leave home.

Emergency Whistle + Space Blanket

A whistle carries farther than your voice and works when you're too exhausted to yell. A space blanket weighs 2 oz and can prevent hypothermia — WA's most common backcountry killer. Both fit in a shirt pocket.

camping Shelter & Sleep

⛺

4-season tent or quality hunting shelter

WA mountains can get snow in September. A 3-season tent is risky in October+.

🛏️

Sleeping bag rated to 20°F or lower

WA high country at night in October can hit 15–20°F. A 0°F bag is not overkill.

🧸

Sleeping pad (insulated, R-value 4+)

Ground sucks heat. An insulated pad is as important as your sleeping bag.

🪣

Tarp or extra rain fly

For covering meat cache, gear shelter, or extra rain prep. WA is wet.

🏮

Camp lantern

A small LED lantern lights up your tent or camp kitchen for evening meals and gear prep. Far safer and more convenient than a headlamp alone at camp.

🪚

Folding saw or axe

For processing firewood and clearing camp debris. A lightweight folding saw handles most WA backcountry needs without the weight of a full axe.

🃏

Playing cards or book

Long evenings in camp are part of the experience. A deck of cards weighs almost nothing and keeps morale high during weather delays or slow hunting days.

outdoor_grill Food & Cooking

🍲

Backpacking stove + fuel canisters

Bring 1 canister per 2 days. Hot food is a massive morale boost.

🫙

Bear canister or hang system

Black bears are common across WA. Protect your food AND your meat.

💧

Water filter + 4L capacity

You'll drink 4–6 liters per day hunting hard. Giardia is real in WA.

🍽️

Main meals

Dehydrated/freeze-dried backpacking meals (breakfast, lunch, dinner — aim for 3,000–4,000+ calories/day). Light, compact, and just need boiling water.

🧀

Additional high-calorie items

Cheese, salami, nut butters, tortillas, instant oatmeal, coffee/tea packets. Dense calories that don't require cooking — ideal for lunch on the move.

🍳

Cook pot, plates, & utensils

Spork, knife, and a small cutting board. A lightweight titanium pot doubles as a bowl. Keep it simple — you're hunting, not camping.

🧼

Biodegradable soap & sponge

Use only biodegradable soap (like Dr. Bronner's) in WA backcountry — regular soap harms waterways. Always wash and rinse at least 200 feet from any water source.

backpack Pack & Carry

You need a frame to carry meat quarters. A frameless pack makes 60-lb quarters torture.

🛡️

Bring at least one full change of base layers and two extra pairs of wool socks per day. Wet clothing is the leading cause of hypothermia in WA backcountry — staying dry keeps you hunting.

Cleaning Your Rifle

Clean your rifle after every use and before each hunting season. Carbon, copper fouling, and moisture cause accuracy loss and corrosion. This takes 20–30 minutes.

What You Need

Cleaning rod with bore brush and patch holder • Cotton patches • Bore solvent (Hoppe's No. 9 or similar) • Gun oil or CLP • Chamber brush • Toothbrush • Clean rags • Muzzle guard (optional but recommended)

Step-by-Step

  1. Make safe. Remove magazine, open the bolt, visually and physically confirm the chamber is empty. Always.
  2. Remove the bolt. Most bolt-action rifles have a release button. Set the bolt aside on a clean rag.
  3. Apply solvent to bore brush. Run the solvent-soaked bore brush through the barrel from breech to muzzle 8–10 times. Never reverse direction mid-stroke — always complete the full stroke before reversing.
  4. Let it soak. Wait 5–10 minutes for the solvent to loosen carbon and copper fouling.
  5. Run patches. Switch to the patch holder. Run solvent-soaked patches through the barrel until they come out clean. This typically takes 5–10 patches. Each patch goes one direction only — muzzle to breech or breech to muzzle, then discard.
  6. Dry patch. Run a dry patch through to remove all solvent. The barrel should look bright and clean when you look through it toward a light source.
  7. Oil patch. Run one lightly oiled patch through the barrel to leave a thin protective film. Do not over-oil — excess oil in the chamber can cause misfires.
  8. Clean the bolt. Use a toothbrush and solvent to scrub the bolt face, extractor, and firing pin area. Wipe dry, then apply a light drop of oil to the bolt body. Do not oil the firing pin channel.
  9. Clean the action. Use a brush to remove loose debris from the receiver. Wipe with a clean rag. Apply a very light coat of oil to metal surfaces.
  10. Clean the chamber. Use the chamber brush with solvent. The chamber must be clean and dry — any oil or debris here affects accuracy and can cause failures to extract.
  11. Wipe down the exterior. Wipe all metal surfaces with a lightly oiled rag to prevent rust. Pay attention to the barrel, scope rings, and any exposed steel on the stock.
  12. Function check. Reinstall the bolt. Dry-fire (safely, with snap caps if you have them) to confirm everything works smoothly before storage.

Pro tip: Before the season, run a dry patch through the barrel after cleaning. Some hunters leave a light oil coat in storage but prefer a dry barrel for hunting — oil on the bullet can slightly affect the first shot's point of impact.

🔫

Cleaning Your Shotgun

Shotguns take more fouling than rifles due to plastic wad residue. Clean after every use — especially when using steel shot which is harder on the bore than lead.

What You Need

Shotgun bore brush (12 or 20 gauge) • Bore mop • Cotton patches • Solvent • CLP or gun oil • Choke tube wrench and brush • Toothbrush • Clean rags

Step-by-Step

  1. Make safe. Open the action, remove all shells, confirm empty visually and physically.
  2. Remove the barrel. Most pump and semi-auto shotguns have easily removable barrels. This makes cleaning much easier.
  3. Remove the choke tube. Use the choke tube wrench to unscrew the choke. Soak it in solvent separately — plastic wad fouling builds up heavily in the choke.
  4. Scrub the bore. Run the bore brush soaked in solvent through the barrel 8–10 times to loosen wad fouling and powder residue.
  5. Use the bore mop. After brushing, run the bore mop soaked in solvent through the barrel — the mop's larger contact area cleans shotgun bores more effectively than patches alone.
  6. Patch until clean. Run patches through until they come out clean and white.
  7. Clean the choke tube. Scrub the choke with the choke brush and solvent. Remove all plastic wad fouling — a dirty choke affects pattern consistency. Dry thoroughly and apply a thin coat of anti-seize compound before reinstalling.
  8. Oil the bore. Run one lightly oiled patch through the barrel.
  9. Clean the action. For pump guns: brush the receiver and action bars. For semi-autos: clean the gas ports and piston assembly (critical for reliable cycling). Consult your owner's manual for semi-auto specifics.
  10. Wipe exterior. Wipe all metal surfaces with a lightly oiled rag. Reassemble.

Steel shot warning: Steel shot is harder on bores than lead. Always verify your shotgun and choke are rated for steel shot before using it. Never use a Full choke with steel shot — use Modified or more open only. This is especially important for waterfowl hunting where non-toxic shot is federally required.

Mounting & Sighting In Your Scope

A scope that isn't sighted in is worse than no scope — you'll miss cleanly or worse, wound an animal. Do this at least 4–6 weeks before the season so you have time to correct problems.

Part 1 — Mounting a Scope

  1. Choose the right rings and base. Match the ring height to your barrel diameter and objective lens size — rings must be tall enough that the scope doesn't touch the barrel. Match the base to your rifle's receiver (Picatinny, Weaver, or proprietary).
  2. Install the base. Clean the receiver top with solvent. Apply blue Loctite to the base screws and torque to spec (typically 15–25 in-lbs). Blue Loctite holds under recoil but can be removed — never use red Loctite on scope mounts.
  3. Set the rings. Place the bottom halves of the rings on the base. Set the scope in the rings before tightening.
  4. Set eye relief. Shoulder the rifle naturally with your eyes closed, then open them. The scope should give you a full, clear picture with no black ring around the edge. Slide the scope forward or back until the image is perfect. This prevents "scope bite" — the scope hitting your eyebrow under recoil.
  5. Level the scope. Use a scope leveling kit or a small bubble level. A canted scope causes your shots to drift sideways as range increases.
  6. Torque the ring caps. Tighten ring cap screws in an alternating cross pattern — do not fully tighten one side before the other. Torque to 15–18 in-lbs. Apply blue Loctite to ring screws.
  7. Function check. Cycle the action several times to confirm nothing interferes. The turrets must be accessible and the scope must not shift during cycling.

Part 2 — Bore Sighting (Before the Range)

  1. Remove the bolt. Look through the barrel from the breech end and center a target at 25 yards in the bore.
  2. Without moving the rifle, adjust your scope's windage and elevation turrets until the crosshairs also center on the same target.
  3. This gets you close enough to hit paper at 25 yards — then you fine-tune at the range. Bore sighting saves ammunition.

Part 3 — Sighting In at the Range

  1. Start at 25 yards. Fire 3 shots from a solid rest (sandbags or a bench rest). Mark where the group hits. Most hunters use a 100-yard zero — starting at 25 yards gets you on paper quickly.
  2. Adjust turrets. Most scopes have ¼ MOA clicks (about ¼ inch per click at 100 yards, or about 1/16 inch at 25 yards). Count how far off your group is in inches and adjust accordingly. "Up" moves the point of impact up; "Right" moves it right.
  3. Move to 100 yards. Once hitting paper consistently at 25 yards, move to 100 yards.
  4. Fire a 3-shot group. Aim at the same point each time. Let the barrel cool between groups — a hot barrel shoots differently than a cold one.
  5. Zero at 100 yards. For most WA hunting (deer, elk at typical distances under 300 yards), zero 1.5–2 inches high at 100 yards. This puts you roughly on at 200 yards and about 4–6 inches low at 300 yards — no holdover needed for most shots.
  6. Confirm zero. Fire a final 3-shot group at 100 yards to confirm. A tight 3-shot group within 1 inch at 100 yards means your rifle is ready.
  7. Check zero after transport. Always fire 1–2 confirmation shots at the start of a hunt if your rifle has traveled by vehicle or been handled extensively — scopes can shift.

First-timer tip: Bring a friend or ask a range officer for help your first time. Most experienced shooters and range staff are happy to help new hunters sight in — don't be afraid to ask. A 20-minute lesson at the range is worth more than any video.

Free Shooting Locations — Washington State

Washington allows recreational target shooting on most DNR, National Forest, and BLM public land where it is safe and legal. You must be at least 150 feet from any road, building, or water. You are responsible for your backstop — always shoot into a solid earthen berm. Pack out all targets and brass.

Rules for all free locations: Never shoot across roads or trails. Ensure a solid earthen backstop behind your target. Clean up all targets, brass, and debris when done. Check for any local fire restrictions before shooting during dry summer months. Shooting is prohibited within 150 feet of any occupied structure, road, or water.

Western Washington — Free

1. Capitol State Forest — Thurston/Grays Harbor County

The most popular free shooting area near Olympia. Thousands of acres of DNR land with established gravel pit areas used regularly for target shooting. Take Hwy 8 west of Olympia, then south on DNR roads into the forest. Use the C-Line or D-Line road areas. No fee, no permit required. Pack out targets and brass.

2. Snoqualmie State Forest — King/Pierce County

DNR land east of Auburn and Enumclaw with gravel pits and clearcut areas suitable for target shooting. Take SR-410 east of Buckley into DNR road areas. Large earthen berms in old gravel extraction areas are ideal backstops.

3. Tahoma State Forest — Lewis County

DNR land near Elbe with logging road access and gravel pit areas. Take SR-706 east of Eatonville into DNR Road 1 area. Popular with hunters preparing for the Lewis County deer season. Discover Pass required for vehicle access.

4. Olympic National Forest — Mason/Clallam County

USFS land on the Olympic Peninsula allows recreational shooting. Use the Hamma Hamma or Skokomish River road corridors. Find a gravel road pullout with a solid hillside backstop. No fee on National Forest land.

5. Willapa Hills State Forest — Pacific County

DNR land in Pacific County with established gravel pit areas and clearcut zones. Take US-101 north of Raymond into the DNR forest road network. Good backstops in old logging cut areas.

6. Mount Baker-Snoqualmie National Forest — Whatcom/Skagit County

USFS land along the SR-20 corridor near Concrete and Rockport. Find a gravel road pullout away from campgrounds with a solid hillside backstop. No fee. Good option for hunters from the Bellingham and Burlington areas.

Central Washington — Free

7. L.T. Murray Wildlife Area — Kittitas County

WDFW Wildlife Area with open shrub-steppe terrain. Find a road cut or draw with a solid earthen hillside backstop on Green Dot roads north of Ellensburg off Reecer Creek Rd. Free with Green Dot permit (free from WDFW).

8. Wenatchee National Forest — Chelan County

USFS land off US-2 east of Leavenworth. Take any forest road into the hills and find a gravel bank or hillside backstop. No fee. Popular with hunters from Wenatchee and the Cascade foothills.

9. Colockum Wildlife Area — Kittitas County

WDFW land north of Ellensburg with open terrain and road cuts providing natural backstops. Access via Vantage Hwy north from Ellensburg. Green Dot permit required.

10. Naches Ranger District — Yakima County

USFS land off US-12 west of Yakima. Take forest roads off the Naches-Tieton Rd into the hills. Multiple hillside areas with solid earthen backstops. No fee on National Forest land.

Eastern Washington — Free

11. Colville National Forest — Stevens County

Millions of acres of USFS land in NE Washington. Take any forest road off US-395 north of Colville into the hills and find a road bank or gravel pit with a solid earthen backstop. No fee. Common practice area for northeast WA hunters.

12. Sinlahekin Wildlife Area — Okanogan County

WDFW Wildlife Area north of Tonasket off Loomis-Oroville Rd. Open terrain with road cuts providing natural backstops. Free access. Good option before hunting north Okanogan.

13. Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest — North — Okanogan County

Vast USFS land off SR-20 near Twisp and Winthrop. Take forest roads into the hills above the Methow Valley and find a solid earthen hillside. No fee. Popular with Methow Valley hunters.

14. Swanson Lakes Wildlife Area — Lincoln County

WDFW land in Lincoln County with open terrain. Take SR-2 west of Davenport and head north into the wildlife area. Coulees and draw edges provide natural backstops. Free access.

15. Columbia Basin Wildlife Area — Grant County

WDFW land in the Columbia Basin with sagebrush coulees providing natural backstops. Take SR-17 north of Moses Lake into the Wildlife Area. Free with standard WA hunting license.

16. Quilomene Wildlife Area — Kittitas County

WDFW land north of Vantage with deep coulees providing excellent natural backstops. Take Huntzinger Rd north from Vantage off I-90. Free access on WDFW land.

17. Umatilla National Forest — Columbia County

USFS land in the Blue Mountains south of Dayton off SR-12. Take forest roads into the Tucannon drainage. Steep hillsides provide excellent backstops. No fee. Good practice location for Blue Mountains elk hunters.

18. Little Pend Oreille NWR — Stevens County

Federal public land east of Colville off SR-20. Road cuts and hillsides on the refuge boundary areas provide shooting opportunities. Check refuge regulations before shooting — confirm open areas with the refuge manager at fws.gov/refuge/little-pend-oreille.

19. Wenas Wildlife Area — Yakima County

WDFW Wildlife Area north of Selah off Wenas Rd. Rolling sagebrush hills with road cuts providing backstops. Free access. Common practice location for hunters targeting the Naches and Oak Creek elk areas.

20. Asotin Creek Wildlife Area — Asotin County

WDFW land in the Snake River country south of Clarkston. Deep canyon draws provide natural backstops. Take SR-129 south of Clarkston into the Asotin Creek drainage. Free access. Popular with SE Washington hunters before deer season.
